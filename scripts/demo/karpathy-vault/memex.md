# Memex

Vannevar Bush's 1945 essay *"As We May Think"* described a personal device he called the **memex**: a desk-sized machine that would store an individual's books, records, and communications, with mechanisms for following *associative trails* between documents. It's the spiritual ancestor of the [[the-core-idea|LLM Wiki pattern]].

## What Bush got right

- **Personal**, not public. The memex was designed for individual use, not as a shared platform.
- **Actively curated** by the user.
- **Associative trails** as first-class objects: the connections between documents are as valuable as the documents themselves.

The web that emerged decades later was almost the opposite of all three: public, automated, and obsessed with documents at the expense of links.

## What Bush couldn't solve

The maintenance problem. Building associative trails is work. Keeping them current as new sources arrive is more work. A single human can't keep up at any meaningful scale.

The LLM-Wiki pattern picks up where Bush stopped. The LLM does the maintenance ([[why-this-works]]). The human does the curation. The wiki is the modern memex.
